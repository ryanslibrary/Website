New EICR Rules for Social Housing: What Landlords in Kent Need to Know

📅 February 2026⏰ 4 min read✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

Electrical safety law has changed significantly. New regulations now bring social housing landlords under the same mandatory inspection regime that private landlords have operated under since 2020 — and the deadlines are already in force.

What’s Changed?

On 25 June 2025, the UK Government laid new legislation before Parliament: The Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) (Amendment) (Extension to the Social Rented Sector) Regulations 2025. In short, this brings social housing providers under the same legal framework as private landlords.

Until now, mandatory Electrical Installation Condition Reports (EICRs) were only a legal requirement in the private rented sector, thanks to the 2020 Regulations. Social housing had no equivalent statutory duty — but that gap is now closed.

Key Deadlines to Know

  • 1 November 2025 – New social tenancies must have a valid EICR in place before the tenancy commences.
  • 1 May 2026 – Existing social tenancies (granted before 1 December 2025) must have completed their first EICR inspection.
  • Within 28 days – Landlords must provide tenants with a copy of the EICR report following any inspection.
  • Within 28 days – Any remedial works identified must be completed within 28 days of the inspection (or sooner if the report specifies).

⚠️ Non-compliance can result in fines of up to £30,000. Local housing authorities now have enforcement powers equivalent to those for private rented properties.

What Does an EICR Actually Involve?

An Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) is a formal assessment of the safety of a property’s fixed wiring — covering the consumer unit, circuits, sockets, switches and earthing. A qualified electrician inspects and tests the installation against current standards and issues a report classifying any issues as:

  • C1 – Danger present. Immediate action required.
  • C2 – Potentially dangerous. Urgent remedial work needed.
  • C3 – Improvement recommended (does not cause the report to be unsatisfactory).
  • FI – Further investigation required.

Why Is This Happening?

The reforms follow a period of heightened focus on social housing safety, including the tragic death of two-year-old Awaab Ishak and the long shadow of Grenfell. Deputy Prime Minister Angela Rayner stated the government is driving “transformational and lasting change in the safety and quality of social housing.”

Planning Permission for EV Chargers Scrapped – What UK Homeowners Need to Know in 2026

📅 January 2026⏰ 3 min read✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

Installing a home EV charger just got simpler. The government has removed planning permission requirements for the majority of residential and commercial EV charger installations — cutting red tape, reducing costs, and making it easier than ever to make the switch.

The Regulatory Change

Confirmed on 29 May 2025 by the Department for Transport and the Office for Zero Emission Vehicles (OZEV), the UK government removed the requirement for planning permission for most EV charger installations.

What Does This Mean in Practice?

For most UK homeowners with off-street parking, EV charger installation now falls under Permitted Development Rights — meaning no planning application is required. On average, this saves up to £1,100 in planning costs.

📌 Exceptions still apply — listed buildings, conservation areas, and properties without off-street parking may still need to check with their local council.

What Rules Still Apply?

  • BS 7671 (IET Wiring Regulations) – earthing, isolation and circuit protection requirements
  • Building Regulations Part S – EV charging infrastructure in new-build homes
  • Smart Charging Regulations – all chargers must be “smart” capable of off-peak scheduling
  • DNO Notification – required for installations above 3.68kW

Arc Fault Detection Devices (AFDDs): The New Technology That Could Save Your Home

📅 December 2025⏰ 4 min read✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

You’ve probably heard of RCDs and MCBs — but there’s a newer type of protective device that’s fast becoming one of the most important weapons against electrical fires.

What Is an AFDD?

An Arc Fault Detection Device (AFDD) is a smart protective device installed in your consumer unit. It uses microprocessor technology to continuously analyse the electrical waveform of the circuit it’s protecting, detecting dangerous arc faults before they can start a fire.

🔥 Arcing electricity can reach temperatures above 6,000°C — more than enough to ignite building materials. Traditional RCDs and MCBs cannot detect this type of fault.

When Are AFDDs Legally Required?

Under Amendment 2 to BS 7671:2018 (in force since 2022), AFDDs are now mandatory in Higher Risk Residential Buildings (over 18 metres), HMOs, purpose-built student accommodation, and care homes.

How Much Do They Cost?

AFDDs are currently priced at around £100 per device, working on a per-circuit basis. The most cost-effective time to install them is during consumer unit work, a rewire, or new build.

Plastic vs Metal Consumer Units: Do You Actually Need to Upgrade Your Fuse Board?

📅 November 2025⏰ 4 min read✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

If you’ve had an EICR or spoken to an electrician recently, you may have been told your plastic consumer unit needs replacing with a metal one. But is that actually the law?

Why Did the Rules Change?

The push for metal consumer units started with Amendment 3 of the 17th Edition of BS 7671, introduced in 2015. Current regulations under the 18th Edition require that any new or replacement consumer unit in a domestic property must be housed in a non-combustible (typically metal) enclosure.

Is My Plastic Consumer Unit Illegal?

BS 7671 is not statute law. Having a plastic consumer unit does not mean your installation is automatically illegal or unsafe. If it was compliant when installed, it can legally remain in service.

💡 An EICR is a condition report, not a pass/fail. A C3 code means “improvement recommended” — it doesn’t mean your property is unsafe or illegal. Only C1 or C2 codes require action.

What Should a Modern Consumer Unit Include?

  • Metal (non-combustible) enclosure
  • RCD or RCBO protection on most circuits
  • Surge Protection Device (SPD)
  • Clearly labelled circuits

How Much Does a Full House Rewire Cost in Kent in 2026?

📅 February 2026⏰ 5 min read✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

A full rewire is one of the biggest electrical jobs you’ll ever have done on your home. Prices vary widely — and some quotes you’ll see online bear little relation to what you’ll actually pay in Kent. Here’s an honest, up-to-date breakdown.

What Does a Full Rewire Actually Involve?

A full rewire means replacing all the fixed wiring in your home — the cables running inside your walls, floors and ceilings, plus the consumer unit (fuse board), sockets, switches and light fittings. It’s disruptive work: chasing cables into walls, lifting floorboards, and making good afterwards. A rewire is typically a multi-day job depending on the size of the property.

You’d typically need a rewire if your home still has old rubber-insulated wiring (pre-1960s), aluminium wiring (common in 1960s–70s properties), or if an EICR has flagged the installation as unsatisfactory with multiple C1 or C2 codes.

Rewire Costs in Kent: 2026 Price Guide

The following figures are realistic estimates for Kent — specifically the Sevenoaks, Tunbridge Wells and Tonbridge area — based on current labour and materials costs. They assume a standard property with a new metal consumer unit, RCBOs throughout, and an SPD.

  • 1-bed flat or small terraced house — £2,500 to £3,500
  • 2-bed semi or terraced house — £3,000 to £4,500
  • 3-bed semi-detached house — £3,500 to £5,500
  • 4-bed detached house — £5,000 to £8,000
  • 5+ bed detached or period property — £7,000 to £12,000+

💡 These are guide prices only. The final cost depends on property size, accessibility, the number of circuits required, and whether additional work is needed (e.g. upgrading the incoming supply, adding EV charging circuits, or installing underfloor heating circuits).

What Affects the Price?

Property age and construction: Older properties — particularly Victorian and Edwardian homes common across Sevenoaks and Tonbridge — often have solid walls, making cable routes harder and more time-consuming to chase. This adds to labour costs compared to a modern cavity-wall build.

Number of circuits: A basic rewire covers lighting circuits, ring final circuits for sockets, and a cooker circuit. Adding dedicated circuits for an EV charger, underfloor heating, a garden room or a home office will increase the price.

Making good: Some electricians quote for the rewire only and exclude plasterwork and decoration. Others include making good as standard. Always clarify this in writing before work begins.

Consumer unit specification: A modern consumer unit with RCBOs (individual RCDs per circuit) costs more than one with split-load RCDs, but provides better protection and is easier to manage when a fault occurs. We always recommend RCBOs as standard.

Should I Get More Than One Quote?

Yes — always. For a job of this size, getting two or three quotes is sensible. But don’t automatically go with the cheapest. Check that every quote covers the same scope of work, includes certification (an Electrical Installation Certificate is a legal requirement), and that the electrician is registered with a competent persons scheme such as NICEIC or NAPIT.

An unusually cheap quote often means something is being cut — cheaper materials, fewer circuits, no certification, or an unregistered trader who can’t self-certify the work. Any of these will cause problems when you come to sell the property.

How Long Does a Rewire Take?

As a rough guide: a 2-bed property typically takes 3–4 days, a 3-bed 4–5 days, and a larger 4–5 bed property can take a week or more. You’ll need to be out of the property during the work, or at minimum have access to alternative facilities as the power will be off for significant portions of each day.

Is a Rewire Covered by Insurance?

Not typically. Most home insurance policies don’t cover rewiring unless damage is caused by an insured event (like a fire or flood). Some specialist policies for older properties do include electrical upgrading as part of a claims process, but this is uncommon. It’s worth checking your policy before assuming.

How to Find a Trustworthy Electrician in Sevenoaks & Kent: 8 Things to Check

📅 February 2026⏰ 4 min read✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

Finding a decent electrician should be straightforward. In practice, the trades industry has its share of rogue operators — people who overcharge, do substandard work, or simply disappear after taking a deposit. Here’s exactly what to check before you hire anyone.

1. Check They’re on a Competent Persons Scheme

In England, electricians who carry out notifiable electrical work (consumer unit changes, new circuits, rewires) must either be registered with a government-approved competent persons scheme — such as NICEIC, NAPIT, or ELECSA — or notify your local Building Control before work begins. Registered electricians can self-certify their work and issue an Electrical Installation Certificate. You can verify registration at electricalcompetentperson.co.uk.

An unregistered electrician doing notifiable work without Building Control notification is breaking the law — and you’ll have no certificate to show when you sell or remortgage.

2. Ask for Their Public Liability Insurance

Any reputable electrician will carry public liability insurance — typically £1m–£5m cover. Don’t be shy about asking to see a certificate. If they hesitate or can’t provide one, walk away.

3. Check Google Reviews — Carefully

Google reviews are useful but can be gamed. Look for reviews that describe specific jobs rather than generic praise. Check that the reviewer profiles look genuine (profile picture, review history). A business with 50+ reviews mentioning specific work is more reliable than one with 5 suspiciously glowing reviews from brand-new profiles.

4. Get the Quote in Writing

A verbal quote is worthless if a dispute arises. Get every quote in writing, specifying exactly what’s included: the scope of work, materials, whether making good is included, the certification provided, payment terms, and an estimated completion date.

5. Never Pay 100% Upfront

A reasonable deposit for materials on a large job (10–30%) is normal. Paying the full amount before work starts is not. If an electrician insists on full payment upfront and then goes quiet, you have very little recourse.

6. Check They’re Local and Established

A locally based business with a track record in your area has more to lose from doing poor work. They rely on word of mouth and local reputation. Tradespeople who travel significant distances for every job, or who operate under a generic trading name with no fixed address, are harder to hold accountable.

7. Be Wary of Unusually Cheap Quotes

If one quote is significantly cheaper than the others, ask why. It might mean cheaper materials, fewer circuits than specified, no certification planned, or work done by an unqualified labourer. The cheapest quote for electrical work is rarely the best value over the lifetime of the installation.

8. Check They Issue the Right Paperwork

On completion of notifiable work, you should receive an Electrical Installation Certificate (EIC) — not just a receipt. This is a legal document. Keep it safe; you’ll need it when you sell. If an electrician can’t or won’t issue one, that’s a serious red flag.

How Much Does an EICR Cost in Kent in 2026?

📅 January 2026⏰ 4 min read✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

EICR prices in Kent range from around £100 to over £400 for the same property. That’s a huge variation — and it matters, because a cheap EICR is often a fast one, and a fast one can miss things. Here’s what you should expect to pay, and what to watch out for.

What Is an EICR?

An Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) is a formal inspection and test of a property’s fixed electrical installation — the wiring, consumer unit, sockets, switches and earthing. A qualified electrician assesses everything against current standards (BS 7671) and issues a report grading any issues from C1 (immediate danger) to C3 (improvement recommended).

EICRs are legally required every five years for private rental properties in England, and from 2025/26 for social housing. They’re also strongly recommended for homeowners buying older properties, or anyone who hasn’t had one in the last five years.

EICR Costs in Kent: 2026 Price Guide

  • 1-bed flat — £100 to £160
  • 2-bed house — £120 to £180
  • 3-bed house — £140 to £220
  • 4-bed house — £180 to £280
  • 5+ bed or larger property — £250 to £400+
  • Commercial premises — priced individually based on circuits and complexity

⚠️ Be cautious of EICR prices significantly below these ranges. A thorough inspection of a 3-bed house takes 2–3 hours minimum. If someone is quoting £79 for a full EICR, ask yourself: how thorough can it really be?

What’s Included in an EICR?

A proper EICR includes a visual inspection of all accessible wiring, consumer unit, sockets and switches; testing of circuits including insulation resistance, earth continuity and polarity; a written report with any defects coded C1–C3 or FI; and the electrician’s overall assessment of whether the installation is satisfactory or unsatisfactory.

It does not include remedial work. If issues are found, you’ll receive a separate quote to fix them.

What Happens If My EICR Fails?

An EICR doesn’t technically “fail” — it’s either satisfactory or unsatisfactory. A satisfactory report can still contain C3 codes (improvement recommendations) without being unsatisfactory. Only C1 or C2 codes make the report unsatisfactory.

For landlords, an unsatisfactory EICR requires remedial work to be completed within 28 days (or sooner if specified), and evidence of completion sent to the tenant and local council if requested.

How Often Do I Need an EICR?

Every five years for rental properties — this is a legal requirement. For owner-occupied homes, every ten years is generally recommended, or at change of ownership. Older properties, or those with known wiring issues, should be inspected more frequently.

Adding a Garden Room or Home Office? Here’s What You Need to Know About the Electrics

📅 January 2026⏰ 4 min read✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

Garden rooms and home offices have boomed across Kent since 2020, and they show no sign of slowing down. But the electrical side is where projects often go wrong — underspecified circuits, missing earthing, no certification. Here’s what to get right from the start.

Does a Garden Room Need Its Own Electrical Circuit?

Yes — always. Running an extension lead from your house is fine for temporary use, but for a permanent garden room used as a workspace, gym, or studio, you need a dedicated circuit from your consumer unit. This means a new circuit breaker in your fuse board, a properly rated cable run to the structure, and a sub-consumer unit inside the garden room itself.

The cable from house to garden room must be either armoured (SWA) for direct burial, or routed in conduit above ground. A standard flex or extension lead buried in the garden is dangerous and non-compliant.

Is Garden Room Electrical Work Notifiable?

Yes. Adding a new circuit from your consumer unit to a garden room is notifiable work under Part P of the Building Regulations. This means it must be carried out by a registered electrician who can self-certify compliance and issue an Electrical Installation Certificate — or you must notify your local Building Control before work begins.

Don’t skip this step. When you come to sell your property, solicitors routinely ask for electrical certificates for outbuildings. Missing paperwork can delay or derail a sale.

How Much Power Does a Garden Room Need?

This depends entirely on how you’ll use it. A simple home office with a laptop, monitor and some lighting needs very little — a 20A or 32A circuit is typically more than sufficient. A garden gym with electric heating, a treadmill and a shower will need significantly more capacity, and possibly a larger incoming cable.

Think ahead. It’s much cheaper to install a slightly larger cable now than to dig it up and replace it in three years because you added a heater and the circuit keeps tripping.

What About Earthing and Bonding?

Garden rooms require their own earthing arrangement. Because the structure is a separate building, it needs an earth electrode (typically a ground rod) or connection back to the main earth, plus equipotential bonding of any metalwork inside. This is a safety-critical requirement that’s sometimes skipped by less experienced installers — don’t let it be missed on your project.

Lighting, Heating and Sockets: What’s Typical?

A well-specified garden room electrical installation typically includes a small sub-consumer unit (4–6 way) inside the structure, a lighting circuit, a ring final or radial circuit for sockets, and a dedicated circuit for heating if required. If you’re planning an air source heat pump, underfloor heating, or EV charging nearby, discuss this upfront so the cable and consumer unit are sized correctly from day one.

How Much Does Garden Room Electrics Cost in Kent?

For a standard garden room installation (new armoured cable run up to 20 metres, sub-consumer unit, lighting, sockets, full certification), expect to pay in the region of £800 to £1,800 in the Kent area depending on cable run length, specification and access. More complex installations with heating circuits or longer cable runs will cost more.
