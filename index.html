New EICR Rules for Social Housing: What Landlords in Kent Need to Know

📅 February 2026 ⏱ 4 min read ✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

Electrical safety law has changed significantly. New regulations now bring social housing landlords under the same mandatory inspection regime that private landlords have operated under since 2020 — and the deadlines are already in force.

What's changed?

The Social Housing (Regulation) Act 2023 and accompanying guidance has extended the requirement for five-yearly Electrical Installation Condition Reports (EICRs) to registered providers of social housing in England. This closes a long-standing gap in the law — private sector landlords have been required to carry out EICRs since 2020, but social housing providers were previously not subject to the same mandatory regime.

Who does this affect?

The new rules apply to registered providers of social housing — this includes local councils, housing associations and other registered providers with residential tenancies in England. If you manage a portfolio of social housing properties in Kent, you need to ensure every property has a valid EICR in place.

An EICR must be carried out at least every five years, or at every change of tenancy — whichever comes sooner.

What are the deadlines?

The government has staggered rollout, but the deadlines are now in force. If you haven't already started reviewing your portfolio for compliance, you need to act now. Failure to comply can result in remedial notices, financial penalties, and reputational damage.

What does an EICR involve?

An EICR is a detailed inspection of a property's fixed electrical installation — consumer unit, wiring, sockets, switches, light fittings and earthing. The inspection is carried out by a qualified electrician and produces a report graded C1 (danger present), C2 (potential danger), C3 (improvement recommended) or FI (further investigation required).

Any C1 or C2 observations must be remedied within 28 days of the report being issued.

Planning Permission for EV Chargers Scrapped — What UK Homeowners Need to Know in 2026

📅 January 2026 ⏱ 3 min read ✍️ Burleigh Electrical Services

Installing a home EV charger just got simpler. The government has removed planning permission requirements for the majority of residential and commercial EV charger installations — cutting red tape, reducing costs, and making it easier than ever to make the switch.

What's changed?

Changes to the Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) (England) Order 2015 now mean that most home EV charger installations are classed as permitted development — meaning you no longer need to apply for planning permission before installation in most cases.

This change applies to standalone EV charging units on houses and flats (including on walls and on structures such as car ports), subject to certain conditions including height limits and placement restrictions.

For the majority of Kent homeowners, you can now have an EV charger installed without any planning application — saving time, cost and paperwork.

Are there any exceptions?

There are some restrictions that still apply. Listed buildings and properties in designated areas (such as conservation areas, Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty, or National Parks) may still require planning permission. If your property falls into these categories, it's worth checking with your local planning authority before proceeding.

Does the charger still need to be installed by a qualified electrician?

Yes — absolutely. While planning permission may no longer be required, an EV charger installation is a notifiable electrical work under Part P of the Building Regulations. It must be carried out by a qualified, registered electrician who can self-certify the work, or notified to building control.
